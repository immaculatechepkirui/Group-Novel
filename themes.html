RELIGION

In “The Handmaid's Tale”, religion is one of the main means of control used by
Gilead. Where the society was heavily influenced by interpretation of the bible.
Gilead claims to promote christianity values such as charity and forgiveness,
while in reality these values are distorted and used as a way to
promote the oppression of women. In the book, people in power use certain
passages from the bible like the phrase “Blessed be the fruit” which is used as a
greeting among handmaids, underscores their sole purpose of
reproduction, reducing their identity to their biological function. This is also still
a big issue in today’s society, where because of religion women are
manipulated to believe they are only meant for some things not the other,
mainly in African societies like Rwanda specifically where some churches
prohibit women from doing certain roles, or having certain rights like how
the bible says women should be submissive. This is used in today’s world
to manipulate women into believing they are meant to be just house wives
who respect and do whatever men say in the form of being respectful to men
no matter how badly they are treated.

In The Handmaid’s Tale the concept of loss of agency refers to the systematic stripping away of individual autonomy and
freedom, particularly women within the oppressive society of Gilead. Gilead’s regime controls women’s bodies, identities,
and decision-making ability by reducing them to vessels for procreation and instruments of state control. Women are
categorized as the handmaids (Tasked with reproduction only), the Marthas (tasked with doing the house chores), and
the Elites (wives to the commanders). The handmaids are assigned to reproduce for the Elites, stripped of their personal
freedom and forced to engage in sexual acts for procreation. The Marthas, Aunts, and Wives are subjected to varying
degrees of control and restriction, lacking autonomy in their lives. This shows the complete absence of reproductive rights
for women in Gilead, who are not allowed to make decisions about their own bodies or future pregnancies. Gilead
systematically eliminates individual names and identities, replacing them with assigned roles that reinforce the societal
hierarchy. Women are denied access to education, employment, and personal property, further limiting their
capacity for independent thought and action. Gilead’s color-
coded attire and strict rules regarding appearance furthercoded attire and strict rules regarding appearance further
emphasize the suppression of individual expression and identity. The women are novice in political decisions or the
legal system. Even seemingly mundane aspects of daily life, like shopping or travel, are controlled by others. The threat of
severe punishment for any deviation from the prescribed rules reinforces the lack of agency and fosters a climate of fear and
compliance. While women in Gilead are largely powerless, the novel also explores subtle forms of resistance such as
maintaining personal memories, relationships, or acts of defiance. The novel’s narration by Offred highlights the
importance of language as a means of resistance and maintaining a sense of self. Even in the face of immense
oppression, the characters hold on to a glimmer of hope for escape and a future where they can exercise their agency.
